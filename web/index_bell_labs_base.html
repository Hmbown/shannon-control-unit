Problem: Pattern-Fitting vs. Structure

Modern AI improvises brilliantly—at growing cost and energy. Teams still need a ruler: a small, auditable measure of structure that works on day one.

Modern AI systems are astonishingly capable. They also cost hundreds of millions to train[1] and consume growing energy—IEA projects data-center electricity ~doubling by 2030, with AI driving half the increase. Shannon offers a complementary primitive: ten lines of code, zero training required.

The current AI paradigm excels at statistical pattern replication. But pattern-fitting and structure-measuring are different primitives. One improvises; the other adjudicates.

Not Anti-AI, Just Orthogonal

Shannon isn't anti-AI; it's orthogonal. If transformers improvise, Shannon adjudicates: a sturdy ruler for structure that scales with data, not parameters. Use it where a ruler belongs.

The ruler we use is older than transformers: compression.

The Primitive: NCD in One Picture + 10 Lines

If two objects share structure, compressing them together saves space. That savings becomes a distance.

def ncd(a, b):
    Ca = compress(a)
    Cb = compress(b)
    Cab = compress(a + b)  # Length-prefixed pair encoding
    return (Cab - min(Ca, Cb)) / max(Ca, Cb)  # Cilibrasi–Vitányi 2005

NCD (Cilibrasi & Vitányi, IEEE Trans. Info. Theory 2005): compressor-based, feature-free similarity metric. Values may exceed 1 slightly due to compressor artifacts.

Known Limits

  • Compressor choice matters; we tune profiles conservatively
  • Short sequences blur the signal; we use windowing and smoothing
  • Cross‑modal similarity is exploratory; workload‑dependent

Failure Transparency

We publish all results, including failures. The hybrid prefilter shows high ΔRecall (>40%) on some datasets like Wednesday workingHours and system_logs, indicating misalignment between the prefilter proxy and anomaly signal. Full failure analysis available in our evaluation results.

This wasn't abstract: it came from a concrete teaching problem and one strange question.

Story: From "Is Math Alive?" to Marching Band

"Claude, super weird question—is math alive?" That curiosity, plus a marching-band trick, led to a ten-line distance that keeps surfacing structure in messy data.

The question pulled me into Kolmogorov complexity—the shortest program that reproduces an object. Meanwhile, teaching 200-person marching band had shown me something profound: naming patterns compresses complexity. When students couldn't handle "Bang Bang" (Jessie J with Ariana Grande & Nicki Minaj, July 2014), I named the parts—"Melody, you are Arianna; Rhythm, you are Thunder"—and suddenly they could carry the structure in working memory. Intelligence isn't accumulating facts; it's compression efficiency.

The Full Naming Moment

  • "Melody—you are now Arianna"
  • "Harmony—you are now Marcus"
  • "Countermelody—you are now Jessie"
  • "Rhythm—you are now Thunder"

Names became compression algorithms for musical parts.

After 72 hours of weekend experimentation, those insights crystallized into NCD—a practical measure of shared structure via compression.
